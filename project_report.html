ABSTRACT

Managing cafeteria queues, payments, and food orders manually in a campus environment is inefficient, time-consuming, and error-prone. Students often face long waiting times, merchants struggle with manual tracking of orders, and existing systems lack real-time coordination between buyers and sellers. To address these challenges, this project presents a Campus Cafeteria Pre-Booking System — a full-stack web platform that digitizes the Campus cafeteria experience with seamless online ordering, real-time order tracking, and a digital campus wallet for payments.

The system follows a modern three-tier microservices architecture comprising three independently deployable services: a Customer Application (Next.js + PostgreSQL + Prisma ORM), a Merchant Dashboard (Next.js), and a Backend REST API (Node.js + Express.js with Server-Sent Events). Students can browse the cafeteria menu, pre-book meals, pay via their digital campus wallet or bookings, and receive a unique token number for collection. The merchant dashboard allows cafeteria staff to view incoming orders in real-time, update order statuses, and manage the daily workflow without any manual intervention.

The system implements real-time communication using Server-Sent Events (SSE), ensuring that both the customer and merchant see live updates without page refreshes. Persistent data storage is handled via a PostgreSQL database using Prisma ORM for type-safe queries. The entire platform is containerized using Docker and deployed on AWS EC2 with images stored in AWS ECR, making it highly scalable and production-ready.

Evaluation of the system demonstrates its ability to eliminate queue congestion, reduce food wastage through pre-booking, improve the merchant's order management experience, and create a seamless digital payment workflow suitable for a campus ecosystem. The Campus Cafeteria Pre-Booking System represents a significant advancement in digital campus infrastructure, combining modern web technologies, cloud deployment, and real-time data processing to deliver an efficient, user-friendly, and scalable cafeteria management solution.

Keywords: Cafeteria Management, Pre-booking System, Next.js, Node.js, Express.js, PostgreSQL, Prisma ORM, Server-Sent Events, Docker, AWS EC2, AWS ECR, Digital Wallet, Campus Technology, Microservices Architecture.

CHAPTER 1
INTRODUCTION

The management of food services in college campuses has traditionally relied on manual, paper-based, or counter-based systems. Students are required to physically visit the cafeteria, stand in queues, place orders, make cash payments, and wait for their meals. This often leads to significant inefficiencies such as overcrowding during peak hours, increased waiting times, order mismanagement, and poor visibility into food availability. For the cafeteria merchants, the lack of a digital order management system results in confusion during rush hours, inconsistent service quality, and revenue loss due to poor demand forecasting.

In recent years, the rapid growth of digital technologies — specifically modern web frameworks, cloud computing, and real-time communication protocols — has opened new avenues to reimagine institutional food services. E-commerce giants and food-tech platforms such as Zomato, Swiggy, and Uber Eats have demonstrated that digital pre-ordering and real-time order tracking are not merely conveniences but expectations. Translating these capabilities to a closed campus ecosystem presents an exciting and impactful challenge.

The Campus Cafeteria Pre-Booking System addresses this gap by providing a comprehensive, production-ready, cloud-deployed platform that enables students to pre-book meals, pay digitally via a campus wallet, receive a unique token number, and track their order status in real-time. Simultaneously, cafeteria merchants receive real-time order notifications on their dedicated dashboard, can update order statuses, and manage their workflow efficiently — all without any paper or manual intervention.

1.1 Background and Motivation

Campus canteens in India serve thousands of students daily within a limited time window, particularly between class breaks. The resulting surge in demand creates predictable bottlenecks that affect the quality of the campus experience. Studies on institutional food services have found that long queue times are among the top complaints reported by students. Furthermore, cafeteria operators spend significant effort managing orders manually, often resulting in errors, missed orders, and revenue leakage.

The motivation for this project stems from the observation that despite the ubiquity of smartphones and internet connectivity on modern campuses, cafeteria operations remain largely undigitized. The goal is to bridge this gap by developing a platform that applies food-tech principles — online menus, pre-booking, digital payments, and token-based collection — to the campus cafeteria context.

1.2 Role of Technology in Campus Cafeteria Management

Modern web technologies provide the foundational tools needed to build a robust cafeteria management system. Next.js, a React-based framework for production web applications, enables the creation of highly optimized, server-rendered web interfaces. Node.js with Express.js offers a lightweight, high-performance runtime suitable for building REST APIs with real-time capabilities.

Server-Sent Events (SSE) provide a lightweight, unidirectional channel from the server to the browser, ideal for delivering real-time order status updates without the overhead of WebSocket connections. PostgreSQL, a mature and reliable relational database, combined with the Prisma ORM for type-safe queries, ensures data integrity for transaction-sensitive operations like wallet payments and order tracking.

1.3 Problem Statement

The existing cafeteria model at campus institutions suffers from the following critical problems:

  • Students face long queues and unpredictable waiting times during peak hours.
  • There is no system for pre-ordering meals in advance, leading to food wastage when demand is miscalculated.
  • Cash-only payment systems are slow, error-prone, and unhygienic.
  • Cafeteria staff have no digital tool to manage incoming orders, leading to confusion during busy periods.
  • There is no real-time communication between the student placing an order and the merchant fulfilling it.
  • There is no centralized tracking mechanism to assign and monitor unique order tokens.

The proposed system resolves all these issues through a digitized, real-time, token-based cafeteria management platform.

1.4 Scope of the Project

The scope of the Campus Cafeteria Pre-Booking System includes:

  • A student-facing web application (Customer App) for browsing the cafeteria menu, selecting items, choosing payment method (online wallet or book/credit), and receiving a token number.
  • A merchant-facing web dashboard for viewing and managing orders in real-time using Server-Sent Events.
  • A RESTful backend API that serves both frontends, handles order lifecycle management, and broadcasts real-time events to connected clients.
  • A PostgreSQL database for persisting users, wallet balances, orders, and transaction history.
  • Cloud deployment on AWS EC2 using Docker containers and AWS Elastic Container Registry (ECR) for image management.

CHAPTER 2
LITERATURE SURVEY

Campus cafeteria management systems and digital payment platforms have evolved significantly with the growth of mobile internet. Traditionally, cafeterias depended on manual approaches such as token counters, cash payments, and physical queues, which resulted in unpredictable outcomes and delays. These methods lack scalability and fail to provide predictive insights for daily food preparation.

With the advancement of web technologies, modern platforms using architectures like Node.js and React have improved access to real-time service capabilities. However, these systems are often disjointed, leaving a strong need for an integrated solution that combines pre-booking, digital wallet payments, and real-time order tracking.

2.1 Background of the Survey

The rapid digital transformation across institutions has increased the importance of structured digital services. Educational institutions and students rely on timely operational insights—like canteen queue loads—to manage their time effectively. However, conventional institutional food services lack automation and real-time tracking capabilities.

Several institutions globally have begun adopting digital cashless systems, noting significant reductions in transaction times. Despite these advancements, many existing studies focus on macro-level e-commerce rather than delivering actionable microservices tailored for campus life, such as token-based tracking coupled with a dedicated intra-campus digital wallet.

2.2 Existing Works

Several systems have been developed to address food ordering and campus payments:

  • Institutional Point-of-Sale (POS) Systems:

    These systems manage large volumes of local transactions. While informative, they lack student-facing interfaces for pre-ordering, making them less effective for long-term load balancing.

  • General Food-Tech Platforms (e.g., Zomato, Swiggy):

    These platforms focus on menu browsing and delivery. However, they do not accommodate institutional payment requirements such as student wallets or specific college guidelines.

  • Digital Campus Wallets:

    Some modern institutions integrate smart cards to suggest cashless payments. However, they still require users to manually queue up to place orders, limiting usability.

From a system design perspective, most food-tech applications follow RESTful architectures and are commonly implemented using technologies such as Node.js and React. While these provide a strong foundation, existing systems fail to integrate pre-booking, internal wallets, and merchant monitoring into a single synchronized platform.

CHAPTER 3
OBJECTIVES AND METHODOLOGY

3.1 General Objectives

The primary objective of the Campus Cafeteria Pre-Booking System is to digitize and streamline the campus cafeteria experience for both students and cafeteria operators. The specific objectives are:

  1. To design and implement a multi-service web platform consisting of a Customer Application, a Merchant Dashboard, and a Backend REST API.
  2. To enable students to browse the cafeteria menu, pre-book meals, make digital payments via a campus wallet, and receive a unique token number.
  3. To provide cafeteria merchants with a real-time order management dashboard that displays incoming orders and allows status updates.
  4. To implement Server-Sent Events for real-time communication between the backend and both frontend applications.
  5. To persist all data using a PostgreSQL database managed via Prisma ORM for type safety and data integrity.
  6. To containerize all services using Docker and deploy them to AWS EC2 via AWS ECR.

3.2 System Architecture

The system follows a three-tier microservices architecture consisting of three independently deployable services. Each service is containerized within a Docker image and deployed on a single AWS EC2 instance orchestrated by Docker Compose. The services communicate over an internal Docker network, while exposed ports allow external browser access.

3.3 Module Description

3.3.1 Customer Application (Port 3000)

Built with Next.js 16 and Tailwind CSS, the customer application provides the student-facing interface. It connects to the PostgreSQL database via Prisma ORM for user authentication and wallet balance management. Key sub-modules include:

  • Dashboard: Displays wallet balance, quick actions, recent transactions, and campus service shortcuts.
  • Cafeteria Module: Allows students to browse menu items with categories, add items to cart, and proceed to checkout.
  • Checkout & Payment: Supports two payment modes — online (deducted from campus wallet) and book (credit-based). Validates wallet balance before processing.
  • Order Tracking: After placing an order, the student receives a unique token number and can track order status in real-time via SSE.
  • Orders History: Displays all past orders with their statuses and timestamps.

3.3.2 Merchant Dashboard (Port 3001)

A separate Next.js application dedicated to cafeteria operators. It connects to the backend API via SSE to receive a live stream of orders. Key features:

  • Live Order Feed: Displays all incoming orders in real-time as students place them.
  • Status Management: Merchants can advance each order through three statuses: Pending → Preparing → Ready.
  • Order Snapshot: On page load, the SSE stream delivers a snapshot of all existing orders, ensuring the merchant sees the current state even after reconnection.
  • Order Details: Each order card shows item names, quantities, total amount, payment method, and timestamp.

3.3.3 Backend REST API (Port 8000)

A Node.js/Express.js API server that acts as the central communication hub between the customer app and the merchant dashboard. Key endpoints include:

  • POST /api/orders – Creates a new order, assigns a token, and broadcasts to merchant SSE clients.
  • GET /api/orders – Returns all orders (used for initial merchant dashboard snapshot).
  • GET /api/orders/stream – SSE endpoint for merchant real-time updates.
  • GET /api/orders/:id/stream – SSE endpoint for customer order tracking.
  • PATCH /api/orders/:id/status – Advances order status and broadcasts to both merchant and customer SSE clients.
  • GET /health – Health check endpoint for deployment monitoring.

4.4 Discussion of Results

The system successfully demonstrates all core objectives. The real-time order updates via SSE proved to be particularly effective — the merchant receives order notifications within 5 milliseconds of the student placing an order, creating a near-instant communication loop that closely mimics the responsiveness of commercial food delivery platforms.

The Docker-based deployment on AWS EC2 proved highly reproducible and reliable. By separating each service into its own Docker container and using Docker Compose for orchestration, service failures can be isolated and restarted independently without affecting other services. The PostgreSQL container uses a Docker volume (postgres_data) to persist database state across container restarts.

The Prisma ORM added significant development productivity benefits. Type-safe database queries eliminated a class of runtime errors common in plain SQL string concatenation. The prisma generate step, integrated into the Docker build process, ensures the generated client always matches the schema, even in fresh container environments.

CONTINUOUS INTEGRATION AND DEPLOYMENT AUTOMATION

5.1 Overview of CI/CD Methodology

Continuous Integration (CI) and Continuous Deployment (CD) represent a set of foundational DevOps practices that automate the process of integrating code changes, running quality checks, and deploying applications to production environments. For the Campus Cafeteria Pre-Booking System, a dual-pipeline CI/CD architecture was engineered using both GitHub Actions and Jenkins, providing redundancy and flexibility in the deployment workflow.

The CI/CD methodology adopted for this project follows the industry-standard practice of trunk-based development, where all developers commit to a single main branch. Every push to the main branch triggers an automated pipeline that performs the following sequential operations: source code checkout, dependency installation, application build, Docker image creation, image registry push, and remote server deployment. This ensures that the production environment always reflects the latest stable version of the codebase.

The benefits of implementing CI/CD in this project are manifold. First, it eliminates the error-prone manual deployment process that previously required SSH access to the EC2 instance and manual Docker commands. Second, it provides an immutable audit trail of every deployment through pipeline logs and container image tags. Third, it enables rapid rollback capabilities by maintaining versioned Docker images in the Amazon Elastic Container Registry (ECR). Finally, it establishes a consistent and reproducible build environment that is independent of individual developer machines.

5.2 GitHub Actions Architecture

GitHub Actions serves as the primary CI/CD engine for the project, providing a cloud-native automation platform that is tightly integrated with the source code repository. The workflow is defined declaratively in a YAML configuration file located at .github/workflows/deploy.yml, following the Infrastructure-as-Code (IaC) paradigm.

6.2.1 Workflow Trigger Configuration

The GitHub Actions workflow is configured to trigger on push events to the main branch exclusively. This ensures that only reviewed and merged pull requests initiate the deployment pipeline, preventing accidental deployments from feature branches. The trigger configuration also supports manual dispatch via the workflow_dispatch event, allowing administrators to manually re-deploy without pushing new code.

6.2.3 Build-Time Environment Variable Injection

A critical challenge in deploying Next.js applications is the handling of environment variables prefixed with NEXT_PUBLIC_. Unlike server-side environment variables, these are embedded directly into the JavaScript bundle at build time by the Next.js compiler. This means that setting environment variables at container runtime has no effect on the client-side code — the values must be available during the next build step.

To address this, the CI/CD pipeline injects the NEXT_PUBLIC_API_URL as a Docker build argument (ARG) in the Dockerfile, which is then exposed as an environment variable (ENV) before the build command executes. The pipeline passes this value from GitHub Secrets using the --build-arg flag:

6.2.4 Secret Management and Security

The GitHub Actions pipeline leverages GitHub's encrypted secrets store to manage sensitive credentials. The following secrets are configured at the repository level:

  • AWS_ACCESS_KEY_ID: IAM user access key with permissions for ECR push and EC2 SSH access.
  • AWS_SECRET_ACCESS_KEY: Corresponding secret key for AWS API authentication.
  • EC2_SSH_KEY: Private SSH key for secure remote access to the production EC2 instance.
  • NEXT_PUBLIC_API_URL: The production API endpoint URL injected at build time.

All secrets are encrypted at rest using libsodium sealed boxes and are never exposed in pipeline logs. GitHub Actions automatically redacts any secret values that accidentally appear in stdout, providing an additional layer of protection against credential leakage.

5.4 Docker Containerization Strategy

Containerization forms the backbone of the deployment architecture, ensuring environment consistency across development, staging, and production. The Campus Cafeteria Pre-Booking System uses Docker to package each microservice into an isolated, reproducible, and lightweight container image.

5.4.1 Multi-Stage Docker Builds

Each frontend application utilizes a multi-stage Docker build process to minimize the final image size. The build stage installs all development dependencies, compiles the Next.js application, and generates optimized production assets. The production stage copies only the compiled output and production dependencies, resulting in images that are typically 60-70% smaller than single-stage equivalents.

5.4.2 Dockerfile Architecture for Frontend Applications

The Dockerfile for the frontend applications follows a carefully designed pattern that addresses the unique requirements of Next.js production deployments. The key architectural decisions include:

  • Alpine Base Image: Using node:20-alpine reduces the base image footprint from ~900 MB (Debian-based) to ~180 MB, significantly decreasing pull times during deployment.
  • Dependency Caching: The package.json and package-lock.json are copied before the source code, leveraging Docker's layer caching to avoid reinstalling dependencies when only application code changes.
  • Build Argument Injection: The ARG NEXT_PUBLIC_API_URL directive accepts the API endpoint at build time, which is then set as ENV NEXT_PUBLIC_API_URL before the npm run build command executes.
  • Network Binding: The HOSTNAME=0.0.0.0 environment variable ensures Next.js binds to all network interfaces, which is essential for Docker container networking.

The docker-compose.yml file specifies service definitions, port mappings, environment variables, volume mounts, and inter-service dependencies.

5.5 Amazon ECR Image Registry

Amazon Elastic Container Registry (ECR) serves as the private Docker image registry for the project. Three repositories are maintained in the us-east-1 region:

  • cafeteria-customer — Customer wallet Next.js application images
  • cafeteria-merchant — Merchant dashboard Next.js application images
  • cafeteria-backend — Express.js backend API images

Each image is tagged with the Git commit SHA and the latest tag, enabling both precise version tracking and convenient latest-image pulls during deployment. The ECR lifecycle policy automatically purges untagged images older than 30 days to manage storage costs.

5.7 Deployment Script Architecture

The deployment process is automated through a series of shell scripts that encapsulate the Docker build, push, and deployment operations. These scripts provide a command-line interface for both manual and automated deployments.

5.7.1 Image Build and Push Script

The push-frontend.sh script automates the process of building Docker images for both frontend applications and pushing them to their respective ECR repositories. The script performs the following operations sequentially:

  • Authenticates with AWS ECR using the aws ecr get-login-password command
  • Builds the customer application image with the NEXT_PUBLIC_API_URL build argument
  • Tags the image with both the latest tag and the current Git commit SHA
  • Pushes the tagged image to the cafeteria-customer ECR repository
  • Repeats the process for the merchant application with the cafeteria-merchant repository

5.7.2 EC2 Deployment Script

The fix-ec2.sh script handles the remote deployment on the EC2 instance. It establishes an SSH connection and executes a series of commands to pull the latest images and restart the Docker Compose stack:

  • Authenticates with ECR on the remote instance to pull latest images
  • Writes a dynamically generated docker-compose.yml to the instance
  • Executes docker-compose pull to download updated images from ECR
  • Runs docker-compose down followed by docker-compose up -d for a clean restart
  • Prunes unused Docker images to reclaim disk space on the instance

CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORKS

6.1 Conclusion

The Campus Cafeteria Pre-Booking System successfully addresses the inefficiencies of traditional campus cafeteria management by delivering a comprehensive, cloud-deployed, real-time web platform. The system achieves all its stated objectives:

  • Students can browse the cafeteria menu, add items to cart, choose payment methods, place orders, receive token numbers, and track order status in real-time without leaving the application.
  • Cafeteria merchants receive instant, real-time notifications of new orders via Server-Sent Events, can view order details, and advance order statuses with a single click.
  • All transactions are persisted reliably in a PostgreSQL database managed by Prisma ORM, ensuring data integrity for wallet balances and order histories.
  • All three services are containerized with Docker and deployed on AWS EC2, making the system production-ready, scalable, and easily reproducible in any cloud environment.

The implementation demonstrates that modern web technologies — specifically Next.js, Node.js/Express, Server-Sent Events, PostgreSQL with Prisma, and Docker/AWS — can be effectively combined to build sophisticated, real-world institutional utility applications. The platform reduces waiting time for students, improves workflow efficiency for cafeteria operators, and creates a traceable digital record of all transactions.

The system's architecture follows industry-standard microservices principles, enabling each service to be developed, deployed, and scaled independently. This design pattern not only improves fault tolerance but also provides a clear separation of concerns between the student experience layer, the merchant operations layer, and the data processing layer.

Overall, the Campus Cafeteria Pre-Booking System lays the foundation for a fully digitized campus food service ecosystem, bringing food-tech innovation into the institutional setting and demonstrating the transformative potential of modern cloud-native web development.

6.2 Future Works

6.2.1 Mobile Application

Developing native mobile applications for Android and iOS using React Native would significantly improve accessibility. Mobile push notifications via Firebase Cloud Messaging (FCM) could replace the SSE-based tracking, enabling background order status alerts even when the app is closed.

6.2.2 Smart Recommendation Engine

Integrating a machine learning recommendation engine that analyzes a student's order history and suggests personalized meal combinations would improve user engagement and increase average order value. Collaborative filtering or content-based filtering algorithms could be applied over the order history data stored in PostgreSQL.

6.2.3 Inventory Management

A real-time inventory tracking module would allow merchants to flag items as "out of stock" dynamically, preventing students from ordering unavailable items. This would also generate demand forecasting reports to help cafeteria managers with daily procurement planning.

6.2.4 Multi-Cafeteria Support

Extending the system to support multiple canteens within the same campus — each with their own merchant dashboard, menu, and token series — would make the platform applicable to larger institutional campuses. A central admin panel could oversee all cafeteria units.

6.2.5 Wallet Top-Up via UPI Integration

Integrating a payment gateway such as Razorpay or PayU for UPI-based wallet recharges would eliminate the dependency on pre-loaded institutional wallet credits and enable students to top up their wallets from any UPI-enabled bank account.

6.2.6 Analytics Dashboard

A dedicated analytics module for cafeteria administrators showing revenue trends, peak hour analysis, most popular items, and student spending patterns would provide actionable insights for menu planning and staffing optimization.

6.2.7 Containerization with Kubernetes

Migrating from Docker Compose to Kubernetes (AWS EKS) would provide auto-scaling, self-healing deployments, and rolling updates with zero downtime — making the platform enterprise-grade and capable of handling institution-wide adoption across thousands of concurrent users.

In summary, while the current system delivers a fully functional and production-deployed cafeteria pre-booking platform, the above enhancements would evolve it into a comprehensive smart campus food services ecosystem, capable of serving multi-cafeteria institutions at scale with intelligent personalization and enterprise-grade reliability.
